Seed diversity is the range of different crops and crop varieties that exist on farms, in seed banks and in the wild. It is the â€œhidden libraryâ€ inside our food system.

Key idea:

Seed diversity is not only about how many different crops we grow. It also includes how many varieties exist within each crop (for example many types of rice, millets or pulses).

ğŸ§¬ Genetic diversity inside crops

Every seed contains genes that control traits such as size, colour, taste, growth time and resistance to pests or drought. When we have many varieties of a crop, we also have many combinations of these genes.

This genetic diversity gives farmers more options. If the climate changes or a new disease appears, there is a higher chance that at least some varieties will survive.

ğŸ“š Levels of diversity

ğŸ”¹ Between species

Different crops such as rice, wheat, millets, pulses, vegetables and fruits. Growing many species together keeps diets and ecosystems balanced.

ğŸ”¹ Within a species

Many varieties of the same crop. For example red rice, aroma rice and drought-tolerant rice are all different varieties of rice.

ğŸ”¹ On the farm

Diversity in a single field or village. Mixed cropping, home gardens and seed sharing networks all increase diversity in real landscapes.

ğŸš Examples from real crops

Rice

There are thousands of rice varieties with different grain colours, cooking qualities and levels of flood or drought tolerance.

Millets

Finger millet, kodo millet and little millet are examples of hardy crops that can grow in poor soils and low rainfall areas.

Pulses

Black gram, pigeon pea and chickpea provide protein and help fix nitrogen in the soil, improving fertility for the next crop.
