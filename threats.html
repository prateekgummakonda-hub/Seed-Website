Several human activities are reducing the number of traditional seed varieties that still exist.

Why this matters:

When many varieties disappear, our food system becomes more uniform and more fragile. It is harder to adapt to new pests, diseases or climate shocks.

Large-scale farming often uses only a few high-yield varieties over big areas. This can replace local seeds and reduce overall diversity in fields and markets. Uniform fields are also more vulnerable to pests and diseases.

ğŸŒª Climate change

Extreme heat, floods, irregular rains and storms can wipe out sensitive local varieties before they are safely conserved. Sudden shifts in climate can make long-adapted varieties difficult to grow in their original regions.

ğŸ§  Loss of traditional knowledge

When farmers stop saving their own seeds or younger generations move away from farming, knowledge about local varieties and their uses can disappear. Without this knowledge it becomes harder to keep and share diverse seeds.

Poor soil health, chemical pollution and erosion all affect seed quality and plant survival. Degraded soils favour only a few hardy varieties and can push more sensitive traditional crops out of production.
