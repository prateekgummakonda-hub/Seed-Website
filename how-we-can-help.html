ü§ù How we can help protect seed diversity

Everyone can play a role in protecting seeds: students, schools, farmers, local communities and consumers.

Start small:

You do not need to change everything at once. Even one new activity in your school or home can help keep local seeds and knowledge alive.

üè† Everyday choices

  • Choose foods made from traditional grains and pulses (for example millets, red rice or local pulses) when possible.
  • Support local farmers‚Äô markets and small shops that sell diverse crops, not only highly processed packaged foods.
  • Talk with family members about the seeds and recipes they remember from their childhood. Recording these stories helps keep knowledge alive.

üè´ Actions at school

Seed diversity garden

Create a small school garden with different crops and varieties. Students can observe growth, record traits and save seeds at the end of the season.

Awareness projects

Organise posters, presentations, assemblies or exhibitions about seed diversity, climate change and local food cultures.

üåæ Working with farmers and communities

Visits and interviews

Interview local farmers or gardeners about the seeds they use, how they store them and which varieties are disappearing.

Seed sharing events

Support or help organise small seed exchange meetings where people can swap seeds and share the stories behind them.

üíª Using this website

The Seed tools page can be used in class to discuss which seeds match different climates, and how diversity gives farmers more options. The Store locator prototype can help students think about where seeds are actually available in real cities and villages.

You can also use the information on these pages as a starting point for debates, essays or further research projects.
