ğŸŒ Why does seed diversity matter?

Seed diversity supports food security, climate resilience and cultural identity. Losing it makes our food system weaker and less fair.

Big picture:

When farmers and communities keep many seed varieties alive, they are also protecting future options. This gives the next generation more choices than we had, not fewer.

ğŸ¥— Food security and nutrition

Stable food supply

If farmers only grow a few modern varieties, a single disease or climate shock can damage large areas. A mix of crops and varieties reduces the risk of total crop failure.

Diverse diets

Traditional grains, pulses, fruits and vegetables often contain more fibre, vitamins and minerals than highly processed foods. Seed diversity makes it easier for families to eat balanced, healthy meals.

ğŸ’§ Climate resilience

Adapting to change

Some varieties tolerate heat, others survive floods, salinity or drought. When farmers have access to many varieties, they can select the ones that match their local climate conditions.

Soil and ecosystem health

Mixed cropping, rotations with pulses and the use of traditional varieties help protect soil structure, support pollinators and reduce the need for chemical inputs.

ğŸ­ Culture, history and identity

Seeds are part of culture. Many festivals, recipes and stories are linked to specific local varieties. When a seed disappears, a piece of local memory and identity disappears with it.

Farmers, especially Indigenous communities and smallholders, have played a key role in creating and saving this diversity over thousands of years.
